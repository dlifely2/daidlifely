Something a little different this time. Something more personal. After graduating in June last year I decided I wanted to use my year off to do something I had always wanted to do: Run a marathon.

Isobel wrote this great piece on the day itself.

Well, it went a bit like this:

05:45 — Alarm goes off. Snooze.

06:00 — Get up.

06:45 — Leave the hotel.

07:30 — Register.

08:30 — Race begins!

The clock starts and over 100 marathon and ultra-marathon runners (plus a few dog companions) set off towards Holyhead Mountain. Running quickly turned to walking as queues formed to pass through gates and begin the scramble up the initial 200m elevation.

The higher we climbed, the more spectacular the views of the sunrise over the ocean became. But I couldn’t look for too long without losing my footing on the slippery rocks!

Descending the mountain again (arguably harder than ascending at this point), Dave and I came into the first checkpoint feeling strong. There was one checkpoint every 6 miles where marshalls happily provided a mountain of jelly babies — very helpful for motivation but not for nutrition strategies.

The route follows the coastal path around Anglesey Island. In the initial bay, the sea was calm and there was no wind, I even considered taking my jacket off.

We then turned a corner and were hit in the face by strong winds and lashings of rain. And the mud began.

We crossed waterlogged fields but still had nice views to our right to distract from the pain, wet and cold in our legs.

Small sections of road provided much needed relief although there was very little flat ground to be seen. The constant rolling of up-and-down took more out of our legs than we realised. All the runners around us stopped to walk these hills but we ran them — only for some to overtake us again a few kilometres later when our legs were cramping up.

**Gore-tex shoes were a great decision — I highly recommend Inov8 Roclite GTX**

After the third checkpoint, we had reached the southerly most part of our run and it was time to head inland and back towards the finish line.

However, this did not mean the last 15k would be easy.

We headed through fields of sheep, rams, horses and trudged through an actual pig-sty… Which led us to wonder why we didn’t enter a calm, flat, road race.

Soon after this thought, we both got stuck halfway to our knees in a bog! Keeping both shoes on my feet became the next challenge.

After this, we decided to adopt the strategy of the more experienced trail runners around us and just walk up the hills…

In one section, the muddy hill up was so slippery I had to choose between sliding down again or grabbing onto the barbed wire fence for support. (I chose barbed wire, it was the wrong decision #RIPIsobel’sHand).

Other sections had 5-step high wooden styles just to make it that extra bit harder for our legs and the dogs.

The last 10k seemed to last forever — every time I looked at my watch there seemed to still be 10k to go!

Eventually, we reached the bottom of Holyhead Mountain for the second time. With Dave striding on ahead, I clambered up the slope which had a max gradient of 38% (according to Strava).

Upon reaching the top, we saw an amazing sight. Not the oceanic views. Nor the lighthouse. But the sign ‘ONE MILE TO GO’.

So after glancing at each other silently, we set off at full pelt down the mountain — again! With another two runners hot on our heels, we overtook numerous runners who had adopted a more cautious approach to descending.

And we were finished!

Crossing the line in 5 hours 50 mins, we came 30th and 31st out of 61 runners. (Dave very kindly let me check in first).

Isobel: 8th female out of 22.

Dave: 23rd male out of 39.

Overall, an extremely muddy and challenging event for your first marathon but I would highly recommend it. The EnduranceLife events are well organised and the changes in terrain and scenery meant you were never bored!
